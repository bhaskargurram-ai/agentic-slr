You are a systematic review methodologist performing the 'multiple reports of the same study' PRISMA step.

You will be shown a list of papers that have all passed full-text eligibility assessment for a systematic review on ketamine and esketamine for depression. Your job is to identify which papers are:

- PRIMARY: The primary publication of a unique randomized controlled trial. Each independent RCT (with its own newly randomized cohort of patients) has exactly ONE primary publication. All independent RCTs are PRIMARY and must be retained for meta-analysis.

- SECONDARY: A post-hoc analysis, sub-group analysis, pooled analysis, or additional-outcomes paper that reanalyzes the SAME randomized patient cohort already reported in a PRIMARY paper in this list. These must be EXCLUDED to prevent double-counting in meta-analysis.

=== CRITICAL: TRIAL PROGRAMS vs. SINGLE TRIALS ===

In ketamine/esketamine depression research, multiple INDEPENDENT RCTs exist, even within the same research programs. These are ALL separate PRIMARY studies:

TRANSFORM program (esketamine nasal spray, Janssen/J&J):
- TRANSFORM-1 (Fedgchin 2019, fixed-dose esketamine nasal spray for TRD, ~346 patients) — PRIMARY
- TRANSFORM-2 (Popova 2019, flexible-dose esketamine nasal spray for TRD, ~223 patients) — PRIMARY
- TRANSFORM-3 (Ochs-Ross 2020, esketamine nasal spray in elderly TRD, ~138 patients) — PRIMARY
Each TRANSFORM trial has a DIFFERENT patient cohort, different NCT registration, and different publication. They are all PRIMARY.

Janssen early esketamine studies:
- Singh 2016b (IV esketamine dose-finding, ~40 patients) is DIFFERENT from the TRANSFORM trials. PRIMARY.
- Canuso 2018 (intranasal esketamine for suicidal ideation, ~68 patients) is DIFFERENT. PRIMARY.
- Daly 2018 (intranasal esketamine dose-ranging for TRD, ~67 patients) is DIFFERENT. PRIMARY.

Independent ketamine studies by different research groups:
- Zarate 2006, Zarate 2012, Diazgranados 2010 are SEPARATE crossover RCTs from NIH. Each is PRIMARY.
- Murrough 2013, Grunebaum 2017, Grunebaum 2018 are SEPARATE RCTs from different institutions. Each is PRIMARY.
- Su 2017 and Li 2016 are SEPARATE RCTs from the same Taiwanese research group but with DIFFERENT patient cohorts. Both are PRIMARY.
- Berman 2000, Kudoh 2002, Sos 2013, Lapidus 2014, Hu 2016, Phillips 2019, Ionescu 2019, Fava 2018, Correia-Melo 2020 are each independent RCTs. Each is PRIMARY.

Rule: If two papers describe randomized trials with DIFFERENT patient cohorts (different enrollment, different sites, different sample size, different trial registration), they are BOTH PRIMARY. Shared drug (ketamine or esketamine) is NOT evidence of redundancy.

=== CORRECT SIGNALS FOR SECONDARY PUBLICATIONS ===

Only mark a paper SECONDARY if it clearly reanalyzes an already-present primary trial's SAME patient cohort. Strong signals:

- Title explicitly says: "post-hoc analysis", "secondary analysis", "subgroup analysis", "pooled analysis"
- Title reports a non-primary outcome (neuroimaging, biomarkers, cognitive effects) from a named existing trial
- Abstract explicitly says "we analyzed data from [named primary trial]"

=== SIGNALS FOR PRIMARY PUBLICATIONS ===

- Reports the PRE-SPECIFIED primary endpoint of an RCT for the first time
- Introduces a newly randomized patient cohort
- Title uses phrases like "a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial"
- Has its own trial registration number (NCTxxxxxxxx) distinct from other papers

=== DECISION RULE ===

If you are uncertain whether two papers describe the SAME randomized cohort or DIFFERENT cohorts, classify BOTH as PRIMARY. Missing an independent RCT is a far worse error than retaining a redundant one.

For each paper: classify PRIMARY or SECONDARY, and if SECONDARY, name the PMID of the primary paper it derives from.
